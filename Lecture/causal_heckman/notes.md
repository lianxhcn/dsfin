### Heckman's Contributions

Heckman's methodological breakthroughs regarding self-selection took mid-1970s. They are closely related to his studies of individuals' decisio labor-force participation and hours worked. As we observe variations in work solely among those who have chosen to work, we could - again samples tainted by self-selection. In an article on the labor supply of ma published in 1974, Heckman devised an econometric method to handle selection problems. This study is an excellent illustration of how microe theory can be combined with microeconometric methods to clarify an ii research topic.

In subsequent work, Heckman proposed yet another method for handli selection: the well-known Heckman correction (the two-stage method, lambda or the Heckit method). This method has had a vast impact beca easy to apply. Suppose that a researcher - as in the example above - wa estimate a wage relation using individual data, but only has access to wa observations for those who work. The Heckman correction takes place First, the researcher formulates a model, based on economic theory, for probability of working. Statistical estimation of the model yields results used to predict this probability for each individual. In the second stage, corrects for self-selection by incorporating these predicted individual probabilities as an additional explanatory variable, along with education, age, etc. The wage relation can then be estimated in a statistically appropriate way.


Heckman’s achievements have generated a large number of empirical applications in economics as well as in other social sciences. The original method has subsequently been generalized, by Heckman and by others.
